Act as a certificate provider

You may be asked to be a certificate provider to help someone (the donor) when they’re making their lasting power of attorney (LPA).

Providing a certificate for an LPA confirms that in your opinion, at the time of signing:

  • the donor understands the decisions they’ve made in the LPA and its purpose
  • the donor has not been unduly pressured to make it
  • there is no fraud involved in its creation
  • there is no other reason it cannot be created

As a certificate provider helping the donor register their LPA, you would need to:

  • discuss the LPA with the donor
  • witness the LPA
  • prove your identity
  • provide the certificate for the LPA

If you do not want to be a certificate provider

If you do not want to be someone's certificate provider, you can refuse the role. The donor can choose an alternative certificate provider up to the point they sign the LPA.

Who can be a certificate provider

To be a certificate provider, you must be 18 or over and you must either:

  • have known the donor personally for at least 2 years – for example, as a friend or neighbour (but not as a relative)
  • be someone with the relevant professional skills and expertise to provide the certificate for an LPA – for example, a solicitor

You cannot be:

  • an attorney on the donor’s LPA or on any previous LPA or enduring power of attorney, even if it has now been revoked
  • related to or in a relationship with the donor or any of their attorneys
  • a business partner or employee of the donor or their attorneys
  • working in a care home where the donor or one of their relatives lives
  • a director or an employee of a trust corporation acting as one of the donor’s attorneys

Discuss the LPA with the donor

You’ll need to read the LPA and talk to the donor to check they understand it. You should discuss the LPA in private, without attorneys or other people present.

You should consider if they can:

  • understand how the LPA works
  • retain that information long enough to be able to make a decision
  • weigh up the information available to make the decision
  • communicate their decision, for example by talking, using sign language or movements like squeezing a hand

If they seem unusually anxious or are acting differently to how they normally would, consider if they’re being unduly pressured into making the LPA.

If you have concerns about the LPA registration

If you have concerns about the LPA, do not witness it or provide your certificate. Contact the Office of the Public Guardian (OPG) instead.

You can also object to the LPA, which could prevent it from being registered.

If you do not have concerns about the LPA registration

If you do not have concerns about the LPA, you can complete your remaining tasks as a certficate provider.

Witness the LPA

You’ll need to watch the donor sign the LPA. You must confirm you’ve witnessed their signature either:

  • online, by entering a witness code OPG sends you
  • on paper, by signing a paper form

Prove your identity

When you’ve witnessed the donor’s signature, OPG will contact you with guidance about next steps. You’ll need documents or information to prove your identity using GOV.UK One Login. If you haven’t used GOV.UK One Login before, you’ll be asked to set up an account.

You'll also need your certificate provider access code, which OPG will send to you.

If your personal information is incorrect in the LPA, this may prevent it being registered. Contact OPG to amend your details.

Provide a certificate online

When you’ve successfully proved your identity, you’ll be guided through the process of providing your certificate online.

Start now

Provide a certificate by post

If you've received a letter from OPG about being a certificate provider, it should include a paper form. If not, contact OPG and ask for a paper form to be sent to you.

After you’ve completed your role as certificate provider for the LPA

OPG will let the donor know when you have proved your identity and provided the certificate.

Your role in the process is complete.
